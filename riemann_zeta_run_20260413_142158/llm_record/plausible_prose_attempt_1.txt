## Survey of the State

**Known facts:**
- Under RH, lim inf d_n < 0.50895 (proven, 2026).
- Under RH + Montgomery, lim inf d_n = 0 (follows from GUE statistics).
- The GUE spacing density has p_GUE(s) ~ (π²/3)s² near s = 0 (quadratic level repulsion).
- Current methods use mollified second moments of ζ on the critical line to detect small gaps.

**Key gap in the literature:** The barrier at ~0.5 comes from the pair correlation method. The mollifier approach essentially optimizes over a test function that captures pair correlation information with Fourier support restricted to (-1,1) (the range where Montgomery's conjecture is proven). Going beyond this requires either (a) extending the proven range of pair correlation, (b) incorporating triple or higher correlations, or (c) an entirely different mechanism.

## Competing Routes

**Route A (Higher correlations):** Incorporate triple correlation of zeros (Bogomolny-Keating style) to go beyond pair correlation bounds. Problem: triple correlation for ζ zeros is not rigorously established even in restricted ranges.

**Route B (Extreme value via Chen-Stein):** Use the dependent-extremes framework from the cross-domain analogy. This is elegant but essentially recapitulates the GUE prediction — it won't improve unconditional bounds.

**Route C (Semiclassical / prime orbit pairs):** Interesting but too speculative without concrete error estimates.

**Route D (NEW — Mollifier-free approach via resonance method + short Dirichlet polynomials):** Here is my genuinely new route.

## New Route: The Resonance Method for Small Gaps

**Core idea:** Instead of using mollified mean values (which essentially smooth out ζ and detect gaps through second moments), use the *resonance method* of Soundararajan, adapted from its application in large values of L-functions, but now applied to detect *clustering* of zeros.

**New object — the "clustering resonator":**

Define, for a parameter μ ∈ (0,1) and a short Dirichlet polynomial A(s) = Σ_{n≤X} a_n n^{-s}:

$$R_μ(T) = \int_0^T |A(1/2+it)|^2 \cdot W_μ(t) \, dt$$

where $W_μ(t) = \sum_n \mathbf{1}_{[γ_n, γ_n + μ \cdot 2π/\log γ_n]}(t)$ is the indicator that $t$ falls in the first μ-fraction of the gap after the n-th zero.

If lim inf d_n ≥ μ, then W_μ(t) is zero for all sufficiently large t, so R_μ(T) would eventually stop growing. On the other hand, if we can show R_μ(T) → ∞, we prove lim inf d_n < μ.

**The mechanism:** By the explicit formula, W_μ(t) can be expressed in terms of a sum over zeros, and the integral R_μ(T) becomes a bilinear form in the coefficients a_n and contributions from zeros. The resonance method optimizes the a_n to maximize this bilinear form.

The key advantage over the classical mollifier method: the resonance method doesn't require the test function's Fourier transform to be supported in (-1,1). Instead, the coefficients a_n are chosen to *resonate* with the zero distribution at short scales. This is analogous to how Soundararajan's resonance method for large values of ζ circumvents the need for long mollifiers.

**Predicted improvement:** The pair correlation constraint forces the mollifier method to use only the proved portion of Montgomery's conjecture (Fourier support in (-1,1)). The resonance method replaces this with a *one-sided* bound: we only need a lower bound on R_μ(T), which requires only an upper bound on certain sums over pairs of zeros — and these upper bounds are available unconditionally from the work of Goldston-Montgomery and others.

Specifically, I conjecture that optimizing the resonator with X = T^θ for θ slightly larger than 1/2 (using the mean-value theorem for long Dirichlet polynomials, which is known for θ < 1 under RH) should yield:

**lim inf d_n < 0.48** under RH.

The extra savings of ~0.03 over the current bound comes from the resonance method's ability to implicitly use pair correlation information at all scales, not just the proved window.

**Testability:** This conjecture makes a concrete numerical prediction. Moreover, the resonance method optimization is a finite-dimensional variational problem (choosing coefficients a_n for n ≤ X) that can be numerically evaluated.

**Connection to log-gas analogy:** In the Coulomb gas picture, the resonator A(s) plays the role of an external "probe field" that couples preferentially to configurations with small gaps. The optimization of a_n corresponds to tuning the probe to detect thermal fluctuations that overcome the Coulomb repulsion barrier.
