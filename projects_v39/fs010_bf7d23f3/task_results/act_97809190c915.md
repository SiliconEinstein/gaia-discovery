# act_97809190c915 -- Cooper Pair Energy Change at K=0 Verification Log

## Task

Verify the Cooper pair energy change at K=0 with six subtasks:
1. Compute ΔE = E₂ - E₁ = -eV - (+eV) = -2eV with direction (side 1 → side 2)
2. State Cooper pair charge q = -2e
3. Derive electrostatic energy U_j = -2e V_j and electrochemical potential difference Δμ = -2e(V₂ - V₁) = -2eV
4. Confirm agreement: Hamiltonian eigenvalue splitting 2eV matches chemical potential difference 2eV
5. Explain why the factor is 2e (not e) -- Cooper pair nature
6. State sign convention explicitly: which side is at higher potential

## Derivation

### (1) Energy Change ΔE

The Hamiltonian at K=0:

$$H_{K=0} = \begin{pmatrix} eV & 0 \\ 0 & -eV \end{pmatrix}$$

This is a diagonal matrix. The eigenvalue equation Hψ = Eψ decouples:

$$eV \cdot a = E a, \qquad -eV \cdot b = E b$$

Two solutions for non-degenerate case (eV ≠ 0):
- ψ₁ = (1, 0)^T, E₁ = +eV (Cooper pair localized on side 1)
- ψ₂ = (0, 1)^T, E₂ = -eV (Cooper pair localized on side 2)

Energy change for a Cooper pair moving from side 1 to side 2:

$$\Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = -eV - (+eV) = \boxed{-2eV}$$

The negative sign means the Cooper pair **loses** energy: it has lower electrostatic energy on side 2 when V > 0 in the Hamiltonian convention.

### (2) Cooper Pair Charge

A Cooper pair is a bound state of two electrons. Each electron carries charge -e, so the pair carries:

$$q = -2e$$

### (3) Electrostatic Energy and Electrochemical Potential

For a Cooper pair of charge q = -2e at electrostatic potential V_j on side j:

$$U_j = q V_j = -2e V_j$$

The electrochemical potential difference between the two sides (electrostatic contribution for Cooper pairs):

$$\Delta\mu = \mu_2 - \mu_1 = U_2 - U_1 = -2e V_2 - (-2e V_1) = -2e(V_2 - V_1)$$

The Hamiltonian parameter V is identified as V = V₂ - V₁ (side 2 at higher electrostatic potential when V > 0). Substituting:

$$\Delta\mu = -2e(V_2 - V_1) = -2eV$$

This exactly matches ΔE = -2eV computed from the Hamiltonian eigenvalues.

### (4) Agreement Confirmation

The Hamiltonian has eigenvalue splitting:

$$E_1 - E_2 = eV - (-eV) = 2eV$$

The magnitude of the electrochemical potential difference from the Cooper pair charge is:

$$|\Delta\mu| = |\mu_2 - \mu_1| = 2|eV|$$

The eigenvalue splitting |E₁ - E₂| = 2|eV| equals |Δμ| = 2|eV|.

The energy change ΔE = -2eV for Cooper pair motion (side 1 → side 2) equals the electrochemical potential change Δμ = -2eV, confirming the Hamiltonian correctly encodes the Cooper pair electrostatic physics.

This agreement is **not accidental** -- it follows from the fact that the Hamiltonian is written in units of energy per Cooper pair. The diagonal entries ±eV are precisely the Cooper-pair electrochemical potentials (up to an additive energy reference).

### (5) Why 2e, not e

The factor of 2 reflects the Cooper pair's two-electron composition:
- A single electron with charge q = -e traversing potential V changes energy by ΔE_single = -eV
- Two electrons (a Cooper pair) with combined charge q = -2e traversing potential V change energy by ΔE_pair = -2eV

The Hamiltonian is explicitly "in units of energy per Cooper pair" (as stated in the problem). The diagonal entries ±eV represent Cooper-pair energies. If it were a single-electron Hamiltonian, the entries would be ±eV/2 with splitting eV, which would not reproduce the 2e factor in the AC Josephson frequency ω_J = 2eV/ℏ.

This 2e factor is experimentally measurable. The Josephson voltage-frequency relation:

$$f_J = \frac{2e}{h} V \approx 483.6\ \text{MHz}/\mu\text{V}$$

involves the factor 2e, not e. This high-precision relation is used in the Josephson voltage standard (SI volt definition), and the 2e factor has been verified to extraordinary precision.

### (6) Sign Convention

In the Hamiltonian:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} eV & K \\ K & -eV \end{pmatrix}$$

- **Side 1** (upper-left entry +eV) has **higher Hamiltonian eigenvalue**
- **Side 2** (lower-right entry -eV) has **lower Hamiltonian eigenvalue**
- For a positively defined V > 0: E₁ = +eV > E₂ = -eV
- Since the Cooper pair has negative charge (q = -2e), higher electrostatic energy corresponds to **lower** electrostatic potential: U = -2e V_j
- E₁ > E₂ with q = -2e ⇒ V₁ < V₂ (side 2 at higher electrostatic potential)
- **Conclusion**: The Hamiltonian parameter V ≡ V₂ - V₁. When V > 0, side 2 is at the higher electrostatic potential.
- A Cooper pair moving from side 1 → side 2 releases energy -2eV (energy decreases), consistent with a negative charge moving toward a region of higher electrostatic potential.
